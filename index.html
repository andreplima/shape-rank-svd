Exercises
Exercise 1 — The rank-1 case
Arrange the four vertices so that the polygon is an axis-aligned rectangle with both pairs of sides aligned with the lattice lines. Observe the scree plot and the component heatmaps. How many singular values are meaningfully nonzero? What do the heatmaps for k ≥ 1 look like, and why?
Show hint
An axis-aligned rectangle is a Cartesian product of two intervals, so its coverage matrix factors as an outer product A = uvT — a rank-1 matrix. Exactly one singular value is nonzero (σ0 = ‖A‖F). The heatmaps for k ≥ 1 are pure gray because their entries are at machine epsilon.
Exercise 2 — Breaking separability
Start from the axis-aligned rectangle of Exercise 1. Drag one vertex sideways (keeping it on a lattice joint) to turn the rectangle into a parallelogram. Watch the scree plot as you drag. Which singular values change? Does the spectrum decay quickly or slowly after σ0? Toggle between linear and log scale to get a clearer picture.
Show hint
Shearing introduces a coordinate-mixing constraint (the slanted edge couples x and y), which breaks the outer-product structure. A second singular value σ1 appears, capturing the shear correction. The ratio σ1/σ0 grows as the shear increases. The spectrum still decays quickly — the parallelogram is "almost" rank-1.
Exercise 3 — The triangle
Make three of the four vertices collinear so that the polygon degenerates into a triangle. Compare the singular value spectrum with the parallelogram case. Is the effective rank higher or lower? Enable "Normalize each component independently" and inspect the structure of the higher-index heatmaps — what spatial patterns do you see?
Show hint
A triangle has one non-axis-aligned edge (the hypotenuse), which introduces a coordinate-mixing constraint absent in the parallelogram. The spectrum decays more slowly — more singular values are meaningfully nonzero. The higher-index heatmaps show progressively finer spatial patterns, analogous to higher Fourier modes correcting the low-frequency approximation.
Exercise 4 — Resolution and rank
Fix the polygon as the default parallelogram. Increase nx and ny from 2 to 10 and observe how the effective rank (number of nonzero σk) changes. Does the spectrum become richer or sparser as the lattice gets finer? What does this tell you about the relationship between lattice resolution and the complexity of the coverage matrix?
Show hint
As n increases, more cells straddle the slanted edge, introducing more distinct partial-coverage values. The coverage matrix can support up to min(nx, ny) singular values, and more of them become meaningful at higher resolution. At low n (coarse lattice), the matrix is nearly rank-1 even for a parallelogram, because there are too few boundary cells to generate complex structure.
